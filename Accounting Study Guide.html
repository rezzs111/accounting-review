Chapter 1: Managerial Accounting Basics

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the difference between financial and managerial accounting
  • Identify the primary users of managerial accounting information
  • Recognize the key characteristics of managerial accounting
  • Understand cost behavior patterns

Key Concepts

Managerial accounting provides information for internal decision-making. Unlike financial accounting, which follows strict guidelines and is primarily for external users, managerial accounting is flexible and designed for internal management use.

Financial vs. Managerial Accounting

Financial Accounting Managerial Accounting
External users (investors, creditors) Internal users (managers, executives)
Historical focus Future-oriented
Follows GAAP No mandatory rules
Whole organization Segments or departments

Cost Classification

Costs can be classified in several ways:

  • Variable costs: Change in proportion to activity level (e.g., materials)
  • Fixed costs: Remain constant regardless of activity level (e.g., rent)
  • Mixed costs: Contain both fixed and variable components
  • Direct costs: Can be traced to a specific cost object
  • Indirect costs: Cannot be easily traced to a specific cost object

Chapter 2: Cost Volume Profit Analysis

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the components of CVP analysis
  • Calculate contribution margin and contribution margin ratio
  • Determine breakeven point in units and dollars
  • Calculate target profit
  • Understand margin of safety

Key Concepts

Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis examines the relationship between costs, volume, and profit. It helps managers understand how changes in volume affect costs and profits.

Components of CVP Analysis

  • Sales price per unit
  • Variable costs per unit
  • Total fixed costs
  • Sales volume

Key Formulas

Contribution Margin (CM) = Sales Price - Variable Cost per Unit
Contribution Margin Ratio = Contribution Margin ÷ Sales Price
Breakeven Point (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin
Breakeven Point (dollars) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio
Target Profit (units) = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) ÷ Contribution Margin

Chapter 3: Decision Making

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of relevant costs and benefits
  • Apply decision-making techniques to various business scenarios
  • Analyze special order decisions
  • Evaluate make-or-buy decisions
  • Assess decisions with capacity constraints

Key Concepts

Decision making in managerial accounting focuses on identifying and analyzing relevant costs and benefits for specific decisions.

Relevant Costs and Benefits

  • Relevant costs: Future costs that differ among alternatives
  • Irrelevant costs: Costs that do not differ among alternatives (sunk costs)

Common Decision Scenarios

  • Special order decisions: Whether to accept a one-time order at a special price
  • Make-or-buy decisions: Whether to produce a component internally or purchase it from an outside supplier
  • Keep-or-drop decisions: Whether to continue or discontinue a product line or segment
  • Constrained resource decisions: How to allocate limited resources to maximize profit

Flashcards

Click on a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Managerial Accounting

The branch of accounting that provides internal reports to help managers make decisions within an organization.

Variable Costs

Costs that change in proportion to the level of activity or production. Examples include direct materials and sales commissions.

Fixed Costs

Costs that remain constant regardless of the level of activity or production. Examples include rent, insurance, and salaries.

Contribution Margin

The difference between sales price per unit and variable cost per unit. It represents the amount each unit contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.

Contribution Margin Ratio

Contribution margin divided by sales price, expressed as a percentage. It shows the percentage of each sales dollar that contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.

Breakeven Point

The level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs, resulting in zero profit or loss.

Relevant Costs

Future costs that differ among alternative courses of action. These are the only costs that matter for decision-making purposes.

Sunk Costs

Costs that have already been incurred and cannot be changed by any decision made now or in the future. Sunk costs are irrelevant for decision-making.

Opportunity Cost

The potential benefit that is given up when one alternative is selected over another. It represents the cost of the next best alternative.

Margin of Safety

The excess of actual or budgeted sales over the breakeven sales volume. It indicates how much sales can decrease before the company incurs a loss.

Practice Quizzes

Question 1

Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of managerial accounting?

Correct Answer: c) Strict adherence to GAAP

Explanation: Managerial accounting is not bound by GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles). Unlike financial accounting, which must follow GAAP for external reporting, managerial accounting emphasizes relevance and flexibility to meet internal decision-making needs.

Question 2

A company sells a product for $80 per unit. The variable cost per unit is $50, and total fixed costs are $90,000. What is the breakeven point in units?

Correct Answer: b) 3,000 units

Explanation:
Contribution Margin = Sales Price - Variable Cost = $80 - $50 = $30 per unit
Breakeven Point (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin = $90,000 ÷ $30 = 3,000 units

Question 3

Which of the following costs would be considered relevant in a decision to replace an old machine with a new one?

Correct Answer: c) The expected maintenance costs of the new machine

Explanation: Relevant costs are future costs that differ among alternatives. The original cost of the old machine and its accumulated depreciation are sunk costs (already incurred) and therefore irrelevant. The expected maintenance costs of the new machine are future costs that will differ between the alternatives of keeping the old machine or buying a new one.

Question 4

A company has a contribution margin ratio of 40%. If fixed costs are $200,000, what is the breakeven point in sales dollars?

Correct Answer: b) $500,000

Explanation:
Breakeven Point (dollars) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio
= $200,000 ÷ 0.40 = $500,000

Question 5

In a make-or-buy decision, which of the following would be considered an opportunity cost?

Correct Answer: b) The revenue that could be earned by using the manufacturing facilities for other products

Explanation: Opportunity cost represents the potential benefit that is given up when one alternative is selected over another. In this case, if the company decides to make the component, it gives up the opportunity to use its manufacturing facilities for other products that could generate revenue.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Breakeven Analysis

Sunshine Company manufactures sunglasses. Each pair sells for $50 and has variable costs of $30. The company's fixed costs are $200,000 per year.

Required:

  1. Calculate the contribution margin per unit.
  2. Calculate the contribution margin ratio.
  3. Calculate the breakeven point in units.
  4. Calculate the breakeven point in sales dollars.
  5. If the company wants to earn a profit of $100,000, how many units must it sell?
  1. Contribution margin per unit = Sales price - Variable cost = $50 - $30 = $20 per unit
  2. Contribution margin ratio = Contribution margin ÷ Sales price = $20 ÷ $50 = 0.40 or 40%
  3. Breakeven point in units = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin = $200,000 ÷ $20 = 10,000 units
  4. Breakeven point in sales dollars = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin ratio = $200,000 ÷ 0.40 = $500,000
  5. Target profit in units = (Fixed costs + Target profit) ÷ Contribution margin = ($200,000 + $100,000) ÷ $20 = 15,000 units

Problem 2: Special Order Decision

Oceanview Resort has 30 rooms that are currently unoccupied for the next week. A tour company has offered to pay $60 per room per night for all 30 rooms for the entire week (7 nights). The variable cost per occupied room is $25 per night for cleaning and amenities. The resort's fixed costs are $10,000 per week regardless of occupancy.

Required:

  1. Should Oceanview Resort accept the special order? Show your calculations.
  2. What is the minimum price per room per night that the resort should accept for this special order?

  1. Revenue from special order = 30 rooms × 7 nights × $60 = $12,600

    Variable costs = 30 rooms × 7 nights × $25 = $5,250

    Contribution margin = $12,600 - $5,250 = $7,350

    Since the contribution margin is positive ($7,350), Oceanview Resort should accept the special order. The fixed costs of $10,000 are irrelevant to this decision because they will be incurred regardless of whether the resort accepts the special order.

  2. The minimum price per room per night should cover the variable cost per room. Therefore, the minimum price = $25 per room per night.

    Any price above $25 will contribute to covering fixed costs and generating profit.

Problem 3: Make-or-Buy Decision

Techno Manufacturing currently produces a component for its main product. The annual production is 5,000 units with the following costs:

  • Direct materials: $40 per unit
  • Direct labor: $30 per unit
  • Variable overhead: $15 per unit
  • Fixed overhead allocated: $25 per unit (total fixed overhead: $125,000)

A supplier has offered to sell the component to Techno for $95 per unit. If Techno buys the component, it can use the freed-up capacity to produce a new product that would contribute $60,000 in additional profit. 30% of the fixed overhead costs would be avoided if the component is purchased.

Required:

  1. Should Techno make or buy the component? Show your calculations.

Analysis:

First, identify the relevant costs for the make-or-buy decision:

Relevant costs of making the component:

  • Direct materials: $40 × 5,000 = $200,000
  • Direct labor: $30 × 5,000 = $150,000
  • Variable overhead: $15 × 5,000 = $75,000
  • Avoidable fixed overhead: 30% × $125,000 = $37,500
  • Total relevant cost of making: $200,000 + $150,000 + $75,000 + $37,500 = $462,500

Relevant costs of buying the component:

  • Purchase price: $95 × 5,000 = $475,000
  • Less: Opportunity cost (additional profit from new product) = ($60,000)
  • Total relevant cost of buying: $475,000 - $60,000 = $415,000

Decision:

Since the relevant cost of buying ($415,000) is less than the relevant cost of making ($462,500), Techno should buy the component from the supplier. This decision would save the company $47,500 ($462,500 - $415,000).
